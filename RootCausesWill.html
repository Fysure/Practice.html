Root Causes #1

Cameras that are used to identify and train facial recognition systems do not recognize darker skinned individuals at a disproportionately higher rate compared to lighter skinned individuals.
  • Evidence 1: “Furthermore, skin type was chosen as a phenotypic factor of interest because default camera settings are calibrated to expose lighter-skinned individuals."
  • Explanation 1: Poorly exposed images that result from sensor optimizations for lighter-skinned subjects or poor illumination can prove challenging for automated facial analysis. By labeling faces with skin type, we can increase our understanding of performance on this important phenotypic attribute.
  • Evidence 2: “Default camera settings are often optimized to expose lighter skin, methods to improve dataset composition, feature selection, and neural network architectures”
  • Explanation 2: Because algorithmic fairness is based on different contextual assumptions and optimizations for accuracy, it is inconsistent by nature.

    • Root Causes #2

    Training data is not diverse enough, and needs more samples.

  • Evidence 1: “The description of classification methodology lacked detail and there was no mention of what training data was used.”
  • Explanation 1: This quote refers to the Microsoft’s Face Detect service, which they described as using advanced statistical algorithms that “may not always be 100% precise.”
  • Evidence 2: "Instead, darker skin may be highly correlated with facial geometries or gender display norms that were less represented in the training data of the evaluated classifiers."
  • Explanation 2: The skin color ultimately effects whether or not the face ID will accurately detect person. This largely effects people of color who were less represented in the training data.

    • Implication for Root Causes

    Classification systems performed best for lighter individuals and males overall. The root causes of these issues lie in the data used to train how they identify each person individually. By using more inclusive and larger datasets, we can curb this problem over time.
